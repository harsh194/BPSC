üîÑ Complex Legacy

The impact of European advent on India was complex and multifaceted. While it brought economic changes, technological advancements, and cultural exchanges, it also led to exploitation, social disruptions, and erosion of traditional systems.

The legacy of European colonization continues to shape India's socio-political landscape and its ongoing efforts to reconcile its colonial past with aspirations for progress and development.

üè∞ Hundred Years' War Background
Duration: Approximately 116 years between England and France
Primary Cause: Dispute over succession to the French throne
Trigger: After death of King Charles IV of France in 1328, Edward III of England claimed the throne through his mother's inheritance
Salic Law: According to French law, women or their children were not allowed to inherit
French Response: French nobility installed Philip VI as king
Economic Factor: England's wool trade depended on the French region of Flanders

üèÜ Results and Consequences

France: Emerged as a strong nation-state
England: Became embroiled in internal conflict (the Wars of the Roses)
Feudalism: Weakened significantly
Military Evolution: Standing armies and artillery emerged
Historical Impact: Laid foundations of the modern era in Europe
üáµüáπ Portuguese Arrival in India ‚ñº
‚õµ Vasco da Gama's Historic Journey
First Arrival: 20 May 1498 in his ship Sao Gabriel
Destination: Calicut (now Kozhikode), by sea route from Lisbon, Portugal
Route: Sailed through the Atlantic Ocean around Africa
Guide: Guided by a Gujarati pilot named Abdul Majeed
Significance: This journey marked the first direct sea link between Europe and India
Local Ruler: Zamorin was the ruler when Vasco da Gama reached Calicut and welcomed him
üéØ Why Portuguese Arrived in India ‚ñº

üå∂Ô∏è Spice Trade

Spices such as pepper, cloves, cardamom, and cinnamon were in great demand in Europe

Previously, this trade was controlled by Arab merchants and the Venetians (Italy)

The Portuguese sought to break the Arab monopoly by finding a direct sea route

üõ§Ô∏è Discovery of Sea Route

In 1453, the Turks captured Constantinople, closing the land route from Europe to Asia

Therefore, Portugal attempted to reach India by sea

‚úùÔ∏è Religious Purposes

The Portuguese were inspired by the spirit of the "Crusades"

They wanted to spread Christianity in India and Asia

üëë Political and Imperial Expansion

Portugal was becoming a maritime power at that time

They wanted to establish their colonial empire by coming to India

üèÅ Competition with Rival European Powers

Spain, the Netherlands, France, and England were also seeking new routes and trading opportunities

Portugal wanted to gain an edge by being the first to reach India

üí∞ Economic Benefits and Trade Monopoly

The aim was to reach India directly, eliminate the middleman role of Arabs and Italians

Establish monopoly over the spice trade for maximum profits
